Types of Soil in Odisha

Odisha, a diverse Indian state, boasts a wide range of soil types influenced by its unique geographical and climatic features. The soils in Odisha can be broadly categorized into coastal saline soils, forest-associated soils, and soils with a combination of red and black varieties. Each type presents distinct characteristics and agricultural opportunities, contributing to the state's agricultural diversity and productivity..

           
 Red Soil:

            Red soil, which covers approximately 7.14 million hectares in Odisha, is the most predominant soil type in the state, extending across various districts such as Koraput, Rayagada, Nawrangpur, Malkanagiri, Keonjhar, Ganjam, Kalahandi, Nuapada, Bolangir, Dhenkanal, and Mayurbhanj.
            The distinctive red color of these soils is a result of the high iron oxide content. Soils in the first four mentioned districts are heavy in texture, while the remaining districts have lighter textured soils. These soils typically exhibit angular or sub-angular blocky structures.
            Red soils are primarily characterized by a clay fraction dominated by kaolinites and illites. They tend to be strongly to moderately acidic and have low to medium organic matter content, along with poor water retention capacity.
            These soils are notably deficient in essential nutrients such as nitrogen and phosphorus, and are also highly deficient in micronutrients like boron and molybdenum.
            Red soils have a low cation exchange capacity and exhibit high phosphate and sulfur absorption properties while being deficient in calcium and magnesium. Phosphates tend to become fixed and unavailable to crop plants in these soils.
            Effective strategies for managing red soils include applying insoluble phosphates two weeks before sowing seeds or using a combination of insoluble rock phosphates and single superphosphate in equal proportions (1:1) to optimize phosphate utilization.
            Soil acidity can be corrected by applying lime, while the use of 1 to 2 tons per hectare of paper mill sludge can also address soil acidity issues.
            Certain crops, such as winter vegetables and groundnut crops, may require the application of 10 to 15 kilograms of borax per hectare. Additionally, seed treatment with sodium molybdate at 10 grams per 25 kilograms of seeds can correct molybdenum deficiency in pulses and groundnut crops.
            To meet the sulfur demands of oilseed crops, the application of phosphogypsum at 200 kilograms per hectare is recommended.
            Various crops, including rice, finger millet, minor millets, niger, potato, brinjal, and fruit trees like mango, jackfruit, guava, papaya, and sapota, can be successfully grown in these red soils.
            
            
Mixed Red & Yellow Soil:

            These soils, covering approximately 5.5 million hectares, are the second most extensive soil type in Odisha and are primarily found in the districts of Sambalpur, Bargarh, Deogarh, and Sundargarh.
            In undulating and rolling terrains, these soils form mixed red and yellow catenary associations, displaying variations in depth, texture, and color. They are moderately shallow and coarse-textured, with upland soils being shallower and lighter in texture compared to lowland soils.
            The presence of ferruginous concretions and fluctuating water tables imparts a mixed red and yellow color to the soil. Upland soils are moderately acidic, while lowland soils are slightly acidic and are primarily formed by colluvial deposits.
            Upland soils are deficient in nitrogen and phosphorus, whereas lowland soils, especially those under the Hirakud command area, are medium in phosphate and high in potassium. Upland light-textured soils may lack boron, while lowland soils in a rice-rice cropping system are deficient in zinc.
            Soil acidity can be corrected through liming, making the soil more suitable for agricultural purposes.
            Upland soils are conducive for growing crops such as rice, finger millet, sugarcane, potato, brinjal, tomato, and pointed gourd. Lowland soils are suitable for paddy cultivation following pulses as pyra crops. Additionally, fruit trees like mango, guava, and banana thrive in these soils.
            
            
            
Laterite Soil:

            Laterite soil in Odisha cover an area of 0.70 million hectares and are primarily found in districts such as Puri, Khurda, Nayagarh, Cuttack, Dhenkanal, Keonjhar, Mayurbhanja, and Sambalpur.
            These soils are characterized by a compact vesicular structure and are rich in hydrated oxides of iron and aluminum, with minor amounts of manganese, titanium, and quartz. In certain degraded cases, they display a honeycombed structure, notably in Khurda and Cuttack districts.
            The surface of lateritic soils ranges from loamy sand to sandy loam and is often underlain by a hard clay pan, leading to issues like crusting in upland laterite.
            These soils tend to be slightly acidic to strongly acidic, with a pH range of 4.5 to 5.8, primarily due to the presence of higher amounts of exchangeable aluminum and manganese.
            Lateritic soils are generally poor in fertility and have low organic matter content. Available nitrogen and phosphate levels are low, while potash content is medium. Nitrogen is prone to leaching, and phosphate becomes unavailable due to fixation by iron and aluminum oxides.
            The cation exchange capacity of these soils is low, and the percentage of base saturation is also low. Sulfur is absorbed in the form of pyrites or zinc sulfide.
            Medium and lowland soils adjacent to uplands can experience iron toxicity due to the lateral movement of soluble iron from upper ridges.
            Effective soil management practices, including liming, organic manure application, cultivation of green manure crops, and balanced fertilizer use, along with paper mill sludge application (1 to 2 tons per hectare), can improve crop growth in these acidic soils.
            Groundnuts and pulses can be successfully grown in these soils without liming. Potatoes and other vegetables thrive without the need for liming. The application of a mixture of water-soluble and insoluble phosphatic fertilizers can increase crop yields.
            Soil application of boron and seed treatment with molybdenum can enhance legume yields, while rice, finger millet, minor millets, and sesame grow well with proper fertilizer application.
            Fruit trees such as mango, jackfruit, banana, guava, and sapota flourish in these lateritic soils.
           
           
           
 Coastal Saline & Alluvial Soil:

            Alluvial soil in Odisha with high total soluble salts (EC-4dS/m) are primarily found in a narrow coastal belt in the state, extending 5-25 km inland.
            The salinity in these soils results from the deposition of brackish tidal water from the sea through creeks.
            Approximately 0.254 million hectares of saline soils are distributed across several districts in Orissa, including Balasore, Bhadrak, Jagatsinghpur, Kendrapara, Puri, Khurda, and Ganjam.
            These saline soils are rich in soluble salts, particularly chloride and sulfate, along with sodium and magnesium.
            Soil near Lake Chilika is also affected by salts due to flooding from the brackish lake water in districts such as Puri, Khurda, and Ganjam.
            During the monsoon, subsoil salinity builds up due to a high groundwater table in low-lying areas.
            The texture of these soils is mostly clay to clay loam, with a columnar structure.
            The pH of these soils ranges from 6.0 to 8.0, and their conductivity in summer is between 10-40 dS/m.
            The exchangeable sodium percentage varies from 18 to 27. These soils are rich in nitrogen and potassium but low to medium in phosphorus.
            Saline soils also contain sufficient levels of sulfate, boron, molybdenum, and chloride.
            Crop failure in these soils is often due to various factors, including plasmolysis of germinating seeds and roots, seedling death, reduced uptake of K, Ca, and Mg due to excess Na, toxicity due to boron, and hydrogen sulfide injury.
            During the rainy season, salinity hazards are lower due to the dilution and flushing of soluble salts by heavy rains.
            Kharif rice is the main crop, but early drought and cyclonic sea water inundation can pose risks to rice production.
            Salt-tolerant high-yielding rice varieties like Lunisharee, SR-26B, and Mohan outperform local saline-resistant varieties.
            Salt-tolerant rabi crops such as safflower, mustard, barley, linseed, chilli, sugarbeet, tomato, spinach, and cucurbits thrive in these soils.
            Cotton can be a successful crop in saline soil if managed properly.
            Coastal saline soils with high exchangeable sodium but acidic pH can be reclaimed by applying lime, which replaces some of the exchangeable sodium or hydrogen with calcium.
            Using organic manures, recycling straw, and incorporating green manure crops can reduce salinity and alkalinity while improving soil structure.
            Cultural practices such as cropping on alternate ridges of irrigated furrows with the intermediate furrow left fallow and frequent light irrigation can protect crops from salinity.
            Preventive measures, such as constructing salt embankments, implementing suitable drainage systems, flushing out soluble salts, building sluices across creeks, and creating shelter belts near saline areas, significantly reduce salinity hazards.
            
            
            
Deltaic Soil:-

            Deltaic soils, covering 0.67 million hectares, are primarily found in the deltaic regions of rivers such as Mahanadi, Brahamani, Baitarani, Subarnarekha, and Rushikullya, spanning across districts like Balasore, Bhadrak, Jajpur, Kendrapara, Jagatsinghpur, Cuttack, Puri, Gajapati, and Ganjam.
            The textural composition of these soils varies from coarse sand to clay, depending on the geomorphology of the floodplain and the type of alluvial material carried by river water.
            The structure of these soils may be granular or platy, with the latter type being challenging to work with. Clayey alluvial soils tend to crack when dry and become sticky when wet, making plowing difficult.
            Deltaic soils have a high water-holding capacity, but once waterlogged, they take a considerable amount of time to become plowable. Slow permeability in these soils makes drainage problematic.
            Generally, deltaic alluvial soils are fertile, but their fertility may decrease if not regularly recharged by floods. The pH of these soils ranges from acidic to neutral.
            Coarse-textured deltaic soils are deficient in essential nutrients like nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P), potassium (K), and sulfur (S).
            These soils are suitable for rice cultivation in the kharif season and are also suitable for crops like groundnut, mustard, sesame, potato, and various vegetables during the Rabi season. Groundnut, greengram, and black gram can be successfully grown with the residual soil moisture.
            
            
            
Black Soil:-

            Black soils in Odisha are not commonly found but occur sporadically in specific districts, including Puri, Ganjam, Malkangiri, Kalahandi, Nuapada, Bolangir, Sonepur, Boudh, Sambalpur, Bargarh, and Angul, covering an area of 0.96 million hectares.
            The black color of these soils results from the presence of titaniferous magnetite, humins, bitumins, and other factors. They are formed through the weathering of basic rocks in low-lying areas.
            These soils have a heavier texture, with clay content exceeding 30 percent, and are dominated by smectite clay minerals, which can lead to deep cracks during the dry summer season.
            These soils exhibit an effective depth of more than 90 centimeters and have a high capacity to swell when wet, holding a significant amount of moisture. However, they have slow permeability, making them prone to surface soil erosion.
            The soil pH of these black soils is neutral to alkaline, with the presence of free calcium carbonate nodules in the soil profile. They are rich in calcium but deficient in essential nutrients such as phosphorus, potassium, zinc, and boron.
            Upland rice crops in these soils may suffer from iron deficiency, and groundnut, mustard, and safflower respond positively to sulfur application. Ammonia volatilization is higher in paddy fields.
            Black soils in Odisha experience early soil moisture stress during drought conditions. Ploughing is challenging in either excessively dry or wet conditions.
            Soil management of these soils is complex. Tillage operations should be conducted at the right moisture consistency. Green manuring and the application of bulky organic manures can enhance water infiltration rates. Recycling rice straw improves the soil's aggregability.
            These soils are suitable for growing a variety of crops, including rice, jowar, bajra, maize, Bengal gram, safflower, mustard, and cotton.
           
           
           
 Brown Forest Soil:-

            These soils are found in areas associated with forests and are distributed across districts, including Phulbani, Kandhamal, Rayagada, and parts of Ganjam and Nayagarh, covering approximately 0.17 million hectares.
            They exhibit brown to gray-brown color, have a light texture, and are acidic in reaction. These soils contain medium to high levels of organic matter and nitrogen.
            Phosphorus and potash content in these soils is at a medium level. Most micronutrient levels are high, except for molybdenum.
            Due to sloping terrain, soil erosion is a concern, leading to land degradation. Shifting cultivation is a common practice in these areas, further contributing to land degradation.
            Implementing land shaping and controlling 'podu cultivation' can help improve soil and land management in these regions.
            With proper moisture conservation practices, these soils are well-suited for growing ginger, turmeric, and tapioca. Maize, wheat, and mustard also thrive in this soil.
            On marginal lands, the cultivation of niger can be successful with the use of nitrogen fertilizer.
            Established fruit crops in these soils include jackfruit, mango, guava, and citrus, making them suitable for horticultural crops.
            Social forestry plantations have been effectively undertaken in these degraded soils, aiding in their restoration.
           
           
           
 Mixed Red & Black Soil:-

            These soils are a combination of red and black soil, with black soil occurring in patches within the predominant red soil. The red and black soils are intermixed, with red soils on upper ridges and black soils on lower ridges.
            These soils cover approximately 0.16 million hectares in the western districts of Sambalpur, Bargarh, Sonepur, and Bolangir.
            The soils are light to medium-textured and have a neutral pH. Black soils are rich in calcium, while red soils dominate with an
            iron-rich catenary formation.
            These soils are deep and have a medium fertility status. Lowland soils used for rice cultivation are deficient in zinc.
            Successful cultivation includes rice, sugarcane, maize, ragi, groundnut, sesamum, and various vegetable crops with adequate fertilizer application.
            Soils in Orissa vary due to climate, topography, and parent material, making them highly heterogeneous. This heterogeneity results in low average productivity for many crops.
            To improve crop productivity, it is essential to identify specific soil problems and efficiently manage them.
